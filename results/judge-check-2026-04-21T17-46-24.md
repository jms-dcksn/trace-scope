**Fixed agent output:**

```
For a mid-size U.S. insurer deploying an **agentic AI system for claims triage in 2026**, the top 3 regulatory issues to prioritize are:

## 1) Claims triage must not become an unfair claims settlement practice
Claims triage can affect delay, routing, investigation depth, and whether a claim gets expedited or denied. That means it sits directly in the crosshairs of **state unfair claims settlement practices laws** and related market-conduct standards.

- The NAIC’s Model Bulletin says AI-driven decisions “**must comply with all applicable insurance laws and regulations**,” including laws addressing “**unfair trade practices and unfair discrimination**.”  
- A legal summary of the bulletin notes insurers should implement controls to prevent AI from resulting in “**unfair trade practices and/or unfair claims settlement practices**.”

**Why this matters for triage:** if the agentic system systematically routes certain claimants into slower/manual review, under-investigates some claims, or creates disparate treatment, regulators can view that as claims-handling misconduct—not just a technology issue.

**Useful guidance to cite**
- **NAIC Model Bulletin on the Use of AI by Insurers (2023/2024 implementation materials)**  
  Excerpt: “advanced analytical and computational technologies, including Artificial Intelligence (AI) Systems… **must comply with all applicable insurance laws and regulations**… including those laws that address unfair trade practices and unfair discrimination.”
- **NAIC Model Bulletin / related summaries** emphasizing unfair claims settlement practices risk.

## 2) Build a documented AI governance, testing, and human-oversight program
Regulators will expect the insurer to prove it knows how the system works, how it is controlled, and how it is monitored over time.

The NAIC bulletin establishes expectations for responsible AI use and, in practice, pushes insurers toward:
- governance and accountability,
- risk management,
- validation and testing,
- monitoring for bias/model drift,
- human review/escalation for adverse or unusual outcomes.

The NAIC’s AI page says the Bulletin “**establishes guidelines and expectations to ensure responsible use of AI by insurance companies**,” and the Working Group is developing a regulator-facing evaluation tool for AI use, governance, and risk mitigation.

**Why this matters for claims triage:** agentic systems can take actions across multiple systems; you need logs, escalation rules, exception handling, and documented human-in-the-loop controls.

**Useful guidance to cite**
- **NAIC Insurance Topics: Artificial Intelligence**
  Excerpt: the Bulletin “**establishes guidelines and expectations to ensure responsible use of AI by insurance companies**…”
- **NAIC Model Bulletin implementation materials**
  Focus on “verification and testing methods to identify errors and bias.”
- A claims-focused industry summary notes insurers should have a written AI program with “**governance, risk management, and internal audit functions**.”

## 3) State AI-specific requirements are becoming binding, especially in Colorado and New York
By 2026, insurers need to map their claims-triage AI against the most relevant state rules, not just general insurance law.

### Colorado
Colorado’s AI law can apply to high-risk systems used in “consequential decisions,” and the statute specifically includes insurers in its framework/exemption structure. Colorado’s legislature says an insurer is in full compliance if it is subject to specified insurance laws/rules governing external consumer data, algorithms, and predictive models.

- Colorado’s law is effective in phases, with the major compliance date in 2026.
- Claims adjudication/triage can be treated as a consequential decision when it materially affects access to benefits.

**Citation**
- **Colorado SB24-205 (Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act)**
  Excerpt: “An insurer… is in full compliance with the act if the entity is subject to specified laws governing insurers’ use of external consumer data and information sources, algorithms, and predictive models…”

### New York
NYDFS has issued final guidance on AI and external consumer data/info sources for insurance underwriting and pricing. While that circular is underwriting/pricing-focused, it is a strong signal of NYDFS’s expectations around:
- prohibited discrimination,
- data governance,
- oversight of AI-assisted insurance decisions.

NYDFS announced it “**adopted guidance to protect consumers from unfair or unlawful discrimination by insurers using artificial intelligence**.”

**Citation**
- **NYDFS Circular Letter No. 7 (2024)**
  Excerpt from the DFS release: “adopted guidance to protect consumers from unfair or unlawful discrimination by insurers using artificial intelligence.”
```
